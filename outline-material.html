Vowels

Learn the difference between short and long vowels, and the correct tongue position to produce accurate vowel sounds. Example: /iÀê/ in 'sheep' vs. /…™/ in 'ship'.

Consonants

Focus on consonant sounds often mispronounced. Includes voiced and voiceless pairs. Example: /$\theta$/ in 'thin' vs. /$\eth$/ in 'this'.

Word Stressing

Identify which syllable should be stressed. Correct stress changes the word's meaning and class. Example: 'PREsent' (gift) vs. 'preSENT' (give).

Intonation

Learn the rising and falling rhythm (pitch) in sentences, crucial for conveying emotion and the meaning of questions or statements. Example: Rising pitch for 'Are you coming?'

Final S/ES Sound

Master the three ways to pronounce 's' or 'es' at the end of a word: /s/, /z/, or /$\text{IZ}$/. Essential for plurals and third-person singular verbs. Example: 'cats' (/s/), 'dogs' (/z/), 'kisses' (/$\text{IZ}$/).

Final D/ED Sound

Learn when the 'ed' ending is pronounced /t/, /d/, or /$\text{ID}$/. Key to speaking past tense correctly. Example: 'looked' (/t/), 'played' (/d/), 'wanted' (/$\text{ID}$/).

American T

Understand the 'Flap T' (like a soft 'D' sound) and the 'Glottal Stop' when the letter 'T' is between vowels or in certain positions. Example: 'water' (pronounced 'wader').

Contractions

Learn how to pronounce common contractions like "I'm," "don't," and "you're" to sound more natural and fluent. Example: 'I would have' becomes 'I'd've' or 'I woulda'.

Linking (Link Up)

The technique of connecting two words, for example 'pick up' becoming 'pi-cup'. Makes spoken English flow more smoothly. Example: 'An apple' (pronounced 'a-napple').
